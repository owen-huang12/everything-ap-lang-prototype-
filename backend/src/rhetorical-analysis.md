Step 1: What is rhetorical analysis?
RA is not about what a text says. It's about what the writer is doing, why they made the choices they made, and what those choices accomplish for their audience.
Two completely different questions:
Summary (NOT RA)
Answers: What did the speaker say?
Restates content. Tells the reader what's in the text.
Example: "Malala says education is important."
Rhetorical Analysis (YES RA)
Answers: What is the speaker doing, and why does it work?
Analyzes choices. Explains how the text affects its audience.
Example: "By opening with her own near-death experience, Malala transforms an abstract policy debate into a personal imperative."


What the prompt is actually asking
Every AP Lang Q2 prompt asks the same thing in slightly different words:
The standard prompt
Write an essay that analyzes the rhetorical choices [the speaker] makes to achieve [their purpose].


Your job is to identify the choices, name what they do, and explain why they work for this specific audience in this specific moment.
How RA is different from the other two essays
RA (Q2): Analyze how an author makes their argument. Evidence comes only from the passage.
Argument (Q3): Make your own argument. Evidence comes from your own knowledge and experience.
Synthesis (Q1): Build an argument using multiple provided sources.

Step 2: Map the rhetorical situation
Before you analyze a single choice, you have to know who is speaking, to whom, why, and under what circumstances. This is the foundation. A misread rhetorical situation is the single biggest reason commentary falls apart later.
SPACECAT(M) reference
The rhetorical analysis framework

Watch out for
S

Who is speaking? What credibility, identity, and position do they bring to this moment?
Don't just name them. Explain what makes them the right (or unexpected) person for this message.

What does the speaker want the audience to DO, THINK, or FEEL?

Don't dump every fact you know about the speaker. Only what's rhetorically relevant.
E
Exigence
The spark. The specific event or moment that makes this text necessary NOW.
NOT the same as context. Exigence is the trigger; context is the surrounding conditions.

Choices
What writing strategies (imagery, diction, tone, structure, evidence) does each author use to make their argument persuasive?
Naming a choice isn't analysis. You have to explain what the choice DOES for the argument.
A
Appeals
How does the speaker use ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic) to persuade?
Don't just label the appeal. Explain how it works on this specific audience in this specific moment.
T
Tone
The speaker's attitude toward the subject and the audience. Word choice and syntax create it.
Tone is rarely just one thing. Look for shifts. A speaker can be solemn AND defiant in the same passage.
M
Message
The central argument or claim. What is the speaker saying overall?
Distinct from purpose. Message = what they're saying. Purpose = what they want from you.


Then ask the two questions that drive your analysis
Obstacles and Leverage
OBSTACLES: Where might this audience resist? What makes persuasion hard for this speaker in this moment?
LEVERAGE: What advantages does the speaker have? Shared values, credibility, position, timing?


Obstacles and leverage are where commentary lives. Every body paragraph should answer: "How does this choice address an obstacle, or use the speaker's leverage?"
Obstacles and Leverage
This is where most students stop and where commentary gets stuck. After you map SPACE(M), ask these two questions. They are the hinge between identifying choices and analyzing them.
Obstacles
Where might the audience resist? What makes persuasion hard for this speaker?
Does the audience have a reason to distrust the speaker's identity, position, or credibility?
Does the audience have prior commitments that conflict with the speaker's purpose?
Is the topic emotionally charged in a way that makes calm reception unlikely?
Does the speaker face a structural disadvantage (age, status, outsider position)?
Leverage
What advantages does the speaker have?
Shared values, beliefs, or experience with the audience.
Established credibility or moral authority on the topic.
Timing — does a current event create unique receptivity?
A privileged position to speak (eyewitness, insider, victim, expert).
How to use these in commentary
Every body paragraph should answer one of these:
The two questions
1. How does this rhetorical choice address an audience obstacle?
2. How does this rhetorical choice use the speaker's leverage?


Step 3: Read the text rhetorically
Don't just read for content. Read for choices. Use these three moves:
1. Generate a WHAT question
Before you read, turn the prompt into a guiding question. Example: "What is Malala doing in this speech to convince UN delegates to act on girls' education?" Read with that question active. It keeps you focused on the writer's moves, not the content.
2. Chunk the text by FUNCTION
Divide the text into sections based on what each part DOES, not what it says. You're not looking for paragraph breaks. You're looking for shifts in the writer's strategy.
Example: chunking by function
Chunk 1 — Establishes credibility: Speaker introduces themselves and their right to speak on this issue.
Chunk 2 — Reframes the problem: Speaker shifts how the audience sees the issue.
Chunk 3 — Calls to action: Speaker tells the audience what to do.


3. Use analytical verbs (does, not says)
Once you've chunked, name what each chunk DOES. Use verbs that describe rhetorical action, not summary.
Summary verbs (avoid)
says
shows
tells
writes
talks about
mentions
Analytical verbs (use these)
establishes, undermines
reframes, complicates
destabilizes, reassures
concedes, deflects, foregrounds
indicts, vindicates
positions, recasts


Step 4 — Plan your essay
Once you've chunked the text, your essay almost writes itself. Here's the move:
Chunk-to-thesis
Each chunk becomes a body paragraph. Your thesis is just a sentence that names the chunks together.
The thesis formula
Use this template (then loosen it as you get stronger)
[Context] , [speaker] argues that [purpose] . To do this, [speaker] must overcome [obstacle]  by [chunk 1 strategy], [chunk 2 strategy], and [chunk 3 strategy].


Pick a structure
You have three options. For the timed exam, chronology works best. Here's why:
Chronology (recommended for timed writing): Body paragraphs follow the order of the text. Beginning → middle → end. Easiest to organize under pressure.
Device-driven: Each body paragraph is a different rhetorical choice. Riskier under timed conditions because it requires more planning.
Problem/solution: Useful when the text is structured as a problem the speaker is solving. Works for some prompts, not all.
What weak theses do
They list devices generically: "Thatcher uses pathos, ethos, and logos."
They restate the prompt: "Thatcher uses rhetorical choices to convey her message."
They summarize the text instead of analyzing it: "Thatcher talks about Reagan being a great person."

Three thesis levels
Malala UN speech as the example

Same passage, three versions of the thesis, each more developed than the last. Use the level you can confidently sustain in your essay. A clean Level 1 thesis with strong commentary scores higher than a tangled Level 3 thesis you cannot back up.

Level 1 — Basic (earns Row A)
Sample: "In her 2013 UN speech, Malala Yousafzai uses personal narrative, anaphora, and antithesis to convince UN delegates to act on girls' education."
What it does: Names specific rhetorical choices and connects them to a clear purpose. Earns the Row A point. Gives you a body-paragraph map (one paragraph per choice).
Why it works: Defensible. Specific. Not a restatement of the prompt. Most students can write this version under time pressure.
Sentence frame: 
In [context: when/where/what kind of text], [speaker] uses [choice 1], [choice 2], and [choice 3] to [purpose: do/think/feel].


Level 2 — Developed (sets up Row B 3-4)
Sample: "Speaking at the UN nine months after being shot for attending school, Malala Yousafzai uses personal narrative, anaphora, and antithesis to convince delegates that girls' education is a moral imperative, not a policy preference. To do this, she must overcome delegates' tendency to treat education as one issue among many."
What it does: Adds the obstacle (delegates treat education as one issue among many) and a specific purpose (moral imperative, not policy preference). Now the thesis is doing analytical work, not just listing.
Why it works: Names what the speaker is up against. Body paragraphs can now explain how each choice addresses that obstacle. Sets up genuine commentary instead of restatement.
Sentence frame: 
Speaking [context: when/where/what just happened], [speaker] uses [choice 1], [choice 2], and [choice 3] to convince [audience] that [specific claim, not generic message]. To do this, [speaker] must overcome [obstacle: where audience resists].


Level 3 — Sophisticated (primes Row C)
Sample: "Speaking at the UN nine months after being shot by the Taliban for attending school, Malala Yousafzai must convince delegates that girls' education is a moral imperative, not a policy preference, while also resisting the audience's instinct to reduce her to a victim. By weaving personal narrative, anaphora, and antithesis, she leverages her unique authority as a survivor to reframe education from a development goal into a non-negotiable human right, transforming the very vulnerability the Taliban tried to exploit into the source of her moral power."
What it does: Layers obstacles (moral imperative vs. policy preference + the victimhood trap). Names the speaker's leverage (her authority as a survivor). Identifies a tension (vulnerability becoming power) that can be sustained throughout the essay.
Why it works: The complexity is built into the thesis itself. Body paragraphs naturally inherit that complexity. Row C tends to follow when this kind of thesis is sustained.
Warning: Only attempt this if you can sustain it across all three body paragraphs. A Level 3 thesis with Level 1 commentary will hurt your score, not help it.
Sentence frame: 
Speaking [context], [speaker] must convince [audience] that [specific claim], while also [second obstacle: a competing pressure or expectation]. By [choice 1, choice 2, and choice 3], [speaker] leverages [speaker's unique authority or position] to [reframe / transform / recast something into something else].


How to choose your level
Pick Level 1 if: you're short on time, the passage is dense, or you're not yet comfortable with obstacle/leverage analysis.
Pick Level 2 if: you can clearly articulate the obstacle and you have time to develop it in body paragraphs.
Pick Level 3 if: you've identified a genuine tension in the text, you can name the speaker's leverage, and you're confident you can sustain that complexity through the whole essay.


Three structural options + time management
Three ways to organize your essay
Chronology (recommended for timed writing): Body paragraphs follow the order of the text. Beginning → middle → end.
Device-driven: Each body paragraph analyzes a different rhetorical choice across the whole text.
Problem/solution: Useful when the text is structured around a problem the speaker is solving.

Why chronology wins under timed conditions
It mirrors the order you read the text in, so planning is faster.
It naturally creates a line of reasoning (the writer's argument unfolds, your essay tracks it).
It's harder to lose the thread because each paragraph is anchored to a specific section.
Step 5 — Write the essay
This is where most students lose Row B points. Not because they can't write, but because their commentary stops at identification. Don't do that.
The body paragraph formula
Topic sentence — name the chunk's function and connect to your thesis.
Embed evidence — a short, specific quote from the passage.
Close the loop — explain what the choice DOES for THIS audience given THIS obstacle.
Concluding sentence — tie back to the writer's purpose.
The Commentary Loop (the move that wins Row B)
The formula
This choice DOES [X] for this AUDIENCE because of [Y obstacle].

Weak vs. strong, side by side
Row B 1-2: Names and stops
"Malala uses an emotional tone to convey her passionate beliefs about education. This shows her dedication and helps support her argument."
Names "emotional tone." Then stops. Doesn't explain what it does for the specific audience or why it matters given her obstacles.
Row B 3-4: Closes the loop
"By grounding her call in her own near-death experience, Malala transforms an abstract policy debate into a personal imperative, shifting the frame from political strategy to moral urgency for partisan delegates who might otherwise treat girls' education as one issue among many."
Names the choice. Explains what it DOES. Identifies the audience. Names the obstacle. Shows HOW the choice overcomes it.


A real student model that closes the loop
From a 1-4-1 essay (Saujani prompt)
"Saujani's brief sentences are void of sufficient detail. This redirects the audience's attention from the impact of her actions to the actions themselves."
Notice the move: the student names a specific feature ("brief sentences void of detail"), then explains what that choice DOES ("redirects the audience's attention"), and explains what shift it creates ("from impact to action"). That's the loop closing.


How to handle the conclusion
The conclusion is where many strong essays go off the rails. Stay tethered to the text. Do NOT:
Drift into "since the beginning of time..." generalizations.
Tell a personal story about something tangentially related.
Speculate about what would happen if everyone followed the speaker's advice.
Instead: return to the rhetorical situation and the writer's purpose. Stay in the room with the speaker and audience until the very last sentence.
About Row C (sophistication)
Row C is bonus, not a requirement. Don't chase it with a sweeping opener. Sophistication tends to come naturally when your commentary is precise.
 The cleanest paths are:
Notice a tension in the text (the speaker concedes X yet still argues Y).
Layer audiences (the explicit audience is X, but the message also reaches Y).
Situate the choice in its specific historical or cultural moment.
The three moves of strong commentary
1. Name a specific choice. Not "tone" or "diction." A precise, observable feature of the text.
2. Explain what it DOES. Use an analytical verb. Tells the reader what the choice accomplishes.
3. Connect to audience and obstacle. Why this choice, why this audience, why now?


Time management on test day
You have 40 minutes for this essay
0-10 minutes: Read the prompt. Read the passage. Map SPACE(M) and obstacles in the margins.
10-15 minutes: Chunk the text. Label each chunk's function.
15-20 minutes: Write the thesis using the formula. Sketch your three body paragraph topic sentences.
20-37 minutes: Write three body paragraphs. Close the loop in every paragraph.
37-40 minutes: Conclusion. Stay tethered to the text. No "since the beginning of time" gestures.


Step 6 — Score yourself
Before you turn in any RA, check your essay against these. If you can answer yes to all of them, you're in 4-6 territory.
Row A (Thesis) — 1 point
Does my thesis name specific rhetorical choices, not just general categories like 'pathos' or 'diction'?
Does my thesis connect those choices to a specific purpose?
Is my thesis defensible (not just a restatement of the prompt)?
Row B (Evidence and Commentary) — up to 4 points
Did I correctly identify Speaker, Purpose, Audience, and Obstacles?
After I named each rhetorical choice, did I explain what it DOES for the audience?
Did I connect my analysis to the obstacles the speaker faces?
Did I keep my commentary tethered to the text in every body paragraph (including the last one)?
Did each body paragraph build on the previous one, not just repeat the same point?
Row C (Sophistication) — 1 point
Did I do something complex with the text itself (tension, audience layering, situated context)?
Is that complexity woven through the essay, not just a single gesture?

1-1-0 (2/6): Has a thesis but commentary is mostly restatement.
1-2-0 (3/6): Some explanation of how a choice works, but not consistent.
1-3-0 (4/6): Explains at least one choice well, line of reasoning is mostly clear.
1-4-0 (5/6): Multiple choices explained, line of reasoning consistent throughout.
1-4-1 (6/6): All of the above PLUS genuine complexity (tension, audience layering, situated significance).

Common Mistakes 
These are the six patterns that consistently keep students at a 3 or below. Check your own essay against this list before you turn it in.
1. Naming the device and stopping
"The author uses tone to project a more motivated feeling." That sentence has the shape of analysis but does no actual work. Fix: after you name the device, write the sentence that explains what it DOES for the audience.
2. Restating evidence instead of analyzing it
"This illustrates that..." followed by a paraphrase of the quote. The reader already read the quote. They don't need it summarized. Fix: skip the paraphrase. Go straight to function and effect.
3. Formulaic structure that skips the work
Naming a device, dropping a quote, saying it "helps the claim" without explaining how or why. Fix: every paragraph must answer the Commentary Loop question.
4. Losing the rhetorical situation in body paragraphs
You correctly identified obstacles and audience in your SPACE(M) work. Then you forget about them in the body. Fix: in every body paragraph, name the specific audience and the specific obstacle the choice addresses.
5. Misreading the rhetorical situation
Misidentifying the audience or occasion makes the rest of the analysis weaker, even if the writing is fine. Fix: spend the time on SPACE(M). It's the foundation of everything else.
6. The off-the-rails final paragraph
Many strong essays start strong, then collapse in the conclusion: sweeping generalizations, personal anecdotes about flat tires, speculation about what would happen if everyone followed the speaker's advice. Fix: stay in the room with the speaker and audience until the very last sentence.
