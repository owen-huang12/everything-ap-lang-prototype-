Step 1 — What is the synthesis essay?
The synthesis essay (Q1) gives you 6-7 sources on a single topic and asks you to argue your own position using at least three of them. The sources do real work in your essay: they're not just decoration, they're not just proof. They're voices you put in conversation with each other to build something none of them could prove alone.
How synthesis is different from the other two essays
Synthesis (Q1): Build YOUR argument using at least 3 of the provided sources. The sources are your evidence.
RA (Q2): Analyze how an author makes THEIR argument. Evidence comes only from the passage.
Argument (Q3): Make YOUR own argument. Evidence comes from your own knowledge and experience.
The single most important move in synthesis
Sources taking turns (NOT synthesis)
Each source gets its own paragraph or sentence.
Sources are introduced one at a time. They don't talk to each other.
Reads like a list of who said what.
Caps you at Row B 1-2.
Sources in conversation (THIS is synthesis)
Sources are put in dialogue with each other within the same paragraph.
You show what Source A and Source B together say that neither says alone, and how Source C complicates that.
Reads like a thinker building an argument with help.
Required for Row B 3-4.


The question to ask before every paragraph
What do these sources together say that neither says alone?

How to use the families
AGREEMENT verbs let you stack sources together. 'Source B and Source F together extend Source A's claim about scarcity by...'
DISAGREEMENT verbs let you set sources against each other. 'Source D undermines Source B's optimism by showing...'
COMPLEXITY verbs let you do the most sophisticated work. 'Source D does not refute Source B; it qualifies B's claim by adding the condition that...'

The verb test
If you can replace your verb with "says" or "states" without losing meaning, your verb is too weak. Reach for one of these instead.


Step 2 — Read the prompt and the sources
Synthesis gives you 15 minutes of reading time before you start writing. Use it. Most students read passively (note what each source says). High-scoring students read for FUNCTION (note how each source could WORK in their argument).

Practice with the running example
Vertical farming prompt
Vertical farming, the practice of growing crops in stacked indoor environments, has been proposed as a solution to global food insecurity, urbanization, and climate change. Carefully read the following six sources, including the introductory information for each source. Then synthesize material from at least three of the sources and incorporate it into a coherent, well-developed essay that argues your position on the value of vertical farming.


Reading for function vs. reading for content
Passive reading
Note what each source says.
Underline 'main ideas.'
Result: a list. "Source A says X. Source B says Y."
You walk into your essay knowing what's there but not what to do with it.
Active reading (BEAM thinking)
Note how each source could function in your argument.
Mark each source's stance, stake, and what kind of evidence it provides.
Result: roles. "Source A could give me background. Source B could be my exhibit."
You walk into your essay with sources already cast in roles.


Three diagnostic questions for any source
Who is this writer? What's their stake in this question?
What kind of evidence does this source provide? Statistics, narrative, expert opinion, visual?
Where would this source be most useful in MY argument? Background? Exhibit? Counterargument?

Step 3 — Map sources using BEAM
BEAM is the framework for thinking about sources by FUNCTION instead of by CONTENT. Every source can serve one or more of these four functions. Casting your sources into BEAM roles before you write is the single biggest preparation move for synthesis.

Quick BEAM intro
B — Background: Sources that establish facts, definitions, or context for the discussion.
E — Exhibit: Sources you analyze or interpret as examples of the phenomenon you're discussing.
A — Argument: Sources that take positions you can engage, agree with, or push back on.
M — Method: Sources that provide a framework or way of thinking that shapes your analysis.

The vertical farming sources mapped onto BEAM (sample)
Casting the sources
Source A (industry overview, statistics): Background. Establishes what vertical farming is and how big the industry has become.
Source B (case study of a specific vertical farm): Exhibit. A concrete example I can analyze for what it reveals about the practice.
Source D (op-ed challenging vertical farming's claims): Argument. A position I can engage and complicate.
Source F (cartoon or visual on food production): Could function as Exhibit OR Argument depending on how I use it.


The BEAM move that matters most
A source can serve different BEAM functions in different paragraphs of YOUR essay. The same source might give you background in BP1 and become an exhibit in BP2. BEAM is not a label you stick on a source; it is a role you cast a source into.

How to use BEAM
In the 15-minute reading period: read each source and ask 'which BEAM function could this serve?' Write B, E, A, or M next to each source.
Most sources can serve multiple functions. Source A might be Background in BP1 and Exhibit in BP2.
Aim for variety. An essay that uses three Argument sources is making three claims and engaging none of them. An essay that mixes B + E + A puts sources in different roles, which makes conversation possible.
If you can't generate a BEAM function for a source, that's information. The source might not belong in your essay.

Step 4 — Plan with line of reasoning
Once your sources are cast into BEAM roles, your essay is mostly built. You need three things before you write: a thesis, a line of reasoning, and a paragraph plan that puts sources in conversation.

Pick three sources minimum, two strong claims, one structure
Three sources minimum: The rubric requires it. Using fewer caps you at Row B 2.
Two strong claims: Two body paragraphs that each put sources in conversation, plus a counterargument paragraph or move.
One structure: Pick a line of reasoning pattern (concession-refutation, weakest-strongest, problem-solution, etc.) before you start writing.

Write a thesis that contains the architecture
The synthesis thesis formula
Although [concession from one set of sources], [your position], because [reason 1 supported by sources X and Y] and [reason 2 supported by sources Z and W].


What each piece does
Concession: Names the strongest version of the opposing view. Forces you to engage complexity from sentence one.
Your position: A defensible claim, not a summary. Should be qualified, not flat.
Reasons (with sources): Each reason becomes a body paragraph. Each reason names which sources support it. Two reasons is ideal.
The four thesis illnesses
These are the patterns that consistently lose the Row A point or set up a weak essay. Diagnose your draft thesis against this list before you write.

Illness #1: The Grocery List
Symptom: Lists reasons without showing how sources connect. "Vertical farming is good because of efficiency, sustainability, and innovation."
Cure: Make the reasons depend on each other. Use 'because,' 'which produces,' 'leading to.' And tie reasons to sources.


Illness #2: The Source Catalog
Symptom: Names sources without taking a position. "Sources A, B, and C all discuss vertical farming."
Cure: Take a stand. The sources are EVIDENCE for your argument, not the subject of your argument.


Illness #3: The Almost
Symptom: Has a concession but stops before explaining the mechanism. "While vertical farming has costs, it has benefits."
Cure: Add the "because." A concession is not enough. The reader needs the mechanism that explains why your position holds despite the concession.


Illness #4: The Shrug
Symptom: Acknowledges both sides and refuses to land. "Both sides have valid points, and the answer depends on context."
Cure: Pick a side. Even a qualified position is better than no position. The rubric requires a defensible claim, and "it depends" is not a claim.

Three thesis levels (vertical farming example)
Same prompt, three versions of the thesis, each more developed than the last. Use the level you can confidently sustain in your essay. A clean Level 1 thesis with strong commentary scores higher than a tangled Level 3 thesis you cannot back up.

Level 1 — Basic (earns Row A)
Sample: "Vertical farms are an impractical solution to global food insecurity, because their high costs and energy consumption outweigh their benefits."
What it does: Takes a clear, defensible position and names two reasons. Earns the Row A point. Gives you a body-paragraph map (one paragraph per reason).
Why it works: Defensible. Specific reasons. Not a summary. Most students can write this version under time pressure.
Sentence frame: 
[Topic of the prompt] is/is not [your position], because [reason 1] and [reason 2].


Level 2 — Developed (sets up Row B 3-4)
Sample: "Although vertical farms offer real solutions to the land scarcity that constrains traditional agriculture, their cost and energy demands prevent them from serving the populations they claim to address, because the same scale that produces efficiency also produces externalities the smaller version did not."
What it does: Concedes (vertical farms DO offer real solutions), qualifies (only to one specific scarcity), and names the mechanism (scale produces externalities). Now the thesis is doing analytical work, not just listing reasons.
Why it works: The qualifier creates a real argument. Body paragraphs can explore which scarcities qualify and which don't. Commentary will naturally explain mechanisms instead of restating.
Sentence frame: 
Although [concession: what's reasonable about the opposing view], [qualified position: where/when the claim holds], because [mechanism that explains why].


Level 3 — Sophisticated (primes Row C)
Sample: "The question vertical farming forces is not whether it works, but which scarcity it addresses: the limited arable land that constrains traditional agriculture, or the limited carbon budget that constrains industrial energy use. Vertical farming solves the first by deepening the second, which means its value depends on which constraint a region cannot afford to violate."
What it does: Reframes the prompt itself. Doesn't argue vertical farming is good or bad. Argues that the real question is which scarcity matters more in a given context. That distinction can be sustained throughout the essay.
Why it works: The complexity is built into the thesis. Body paragraphs naturally inherit it. Row C tends to follow when this kind of thesis is sustained across the whole essay.
Warning: Only attempt this if you can sustain it across all your body paragraphs. A Level 3 thesis with Level 1 commentary will hurt your score, not help it.
Sentence frame: 
The question [topic] forces is not [the simple reading], but [the more nuanced reframe]. [The reframe creates two conditions], which means [the answer depends on which condition matters].


How to choose your level
Pick Level 1 if: you're short on time, the prompt is unfamiliar, or you're not yet comfortable with concession-based theses.
Pick Level 2 if: you can clearly articulate where the claim fails and where it holds, and you have time to develop the mechanism in body paragraphs.
Pick Level 3 if: you've identified a genuine reframe (a way of seeing the prompt that goes beyond agree/disagree), and you're confident you can sustain that complexity through every paragraph.
Step 5 — Write using sources in conversation
This is where most students lose Row B points. Not because they can't write, but because they let sources take turns instead of putting them in dialogue. The Synthesis Move is the fix.

The body paragraph formula
Topic sentence — name the claim and connect to your thesis.
Embed sources in conversation — don't introduce one source per sentence. Put two or three in dialogue within the same passage.
Commentary using The Synthesis Move — explain what the sources together reveal that none of them reveals alone.
Concluding sentence — tie back to your thesis and set up the next paragraph.

The Synthesis Move (the move that wins Row B)
The full formula (for planning)
[Source A] and [Source B] together reveal [insight neither makes alone] because [mechanism]. [Source C] complicates this by [twist].


The everyday version (for writing)
What do these sources together say that neither says alone?


Weak vs. strong commentary
Weak (Row B 1-2)
"Source B affirms vertical farming's space efficiency. Source D undermines this by showing the energy costs are high. This shows that vertical farming is complicated."
Sources take turns. Each gets its own sentence. The 'this shows' commentary just restates that the sources disagree.
Strong (Row B 3-4)
"Source B and Source F together establish that vertical farming addresses a real and growing scarcity (arable land disappearing under urbanization). Source D complicates this not by denying the space efficiency, but by revealing that the solution introduces a different scarcity: the carbon budget needed to power the lights. The tension between Sources B/F and D reveals that vertical farming does not eliminate scarcity, it relocates it."
Sources B and F are in conversation (together establish). Source D enters as a complication, not a contradiction. The commentary names what the sources TOGETHER reveal: scarcity isn't eliminated, it's relocated.


How to handle the conclusion
The conclusion is where many strong synthesis essays go off the rails. Stay tethered to your argument. Do NOT:
Drift into "since the beginning of time..." generalizations.
Bolt on a new claim that wasn't earned by the analysis.
Hedge with 'in the end, both sides have a point.'

Instead: return to your thesis with new clarity. The conclusion is your last chance to land a sophistication move.

Body paragraph scaffold + The Synthesis Move
Row B is where the score is won or lost. Most students lose points here because they let sources take turns instead of putting them in conversation. The Synthesis Move is the move that fixes that.

The body paragraph in 4 parts
Topic sentence — name the claim and link to your thesis.
Embed sources in conversation — at least two sources within the same passage, not one per sentence.
Commentary using The Synthesis Move — explain what the sources together reveal.
Concluding sentence — tie back to your thesis, set up the next paragraph.
Three commentary moves to escape parallel support
If your paragraph reads like 'Source A says X. Source B says Y. Source C says Z,' you're in parallel support, not synthesis. Use these sentence starters to break out:
Together, these sources reveal ...
What [Source X] adds to [Source Y] is ...
[Source Z] complicates this by ...
Taken together, [Source A] and [Source B] ...
The tension between [Source A] and [Source D] reveals ...
Line of reasoning patterns
Pick a structural pattern BEFORE you write. The pattern determines the order of your body paragraphs and gives the reader a sense of building. Body paragraphs without a pattern feel like a list.
The patterns we use
Concession → Refutation: Open with the strongest opposing view, then dismantle it with your evidence.
Weakest → Strongest: Build momentum across paragraphs. Save your most convincing pairing for last.
Cause → Effect: Show how one principle plays out across multiple cases or sources.
Most Important → Supporting: Lead with your strongest claim, then layer supporting evidence.
Problem → Solution: Establish the problem using sources, then build toward your proposed solution.
Comparison/Contrast: Set sources or cases against each other to reveal what each shows.
Definition: Argue for a particular definition of the prompt's key term, then show what follows.
Description: Build a detailed picture of the phenomenon, then argue for what it reveals.
Chronological/Process: Move through stages of a process or moments in a history.
Same thesis, two different lines of reasoning
The vertical farming thesis 'Although vertical farms offer real solutions to land scarcity, their costs prevent them from serving the populations they claim to address' could be built two ways:

Built as Concession → Refutation
BP1: Concede the strongest version of the opposing view. Vertical farms really do solve land scarcity (Sources A and B in conversation).
BP2: Refute by introducing energy costs (Source D), and show the population they claim to serve cannot afford the result.


Built as Weakest → Strongest
BP1: Open with the moderate critique: vertical farms are expensive (Source B's case study, Source D's reporting).
BP2: Build to the stronger critique: the populations facing food insecurity cannot afford vertical farm produce (Sources A, D, F together).

Counterargument: how to actually do it
Counterargument is the most misunderstood move in synthesis. Done well, it's a path to Row C. Done poorly, it actively HURTS your Row B score by suggesting your argument has a hole you couldn't fill.
What NOT to do
DON’T DO: Acknowledge and Dismiss
"Source F suggests positive culture develops over time, but this is not realistic in most schools."
Raises the counterargument. Doesn't engage it. Just dismisses with a flat assertion. Hurts Row B because it shows your argument has a gap.
DON’T DO: Define Your Way Out
"For the sake of this essay, I'm only counting the cases where vertical farms work."
Circular. You're saying you'll only count the evidence that proves your point. Readers see this immediately.


How to refute well
Steel-man it. State the strongest version of the opposing view, often using the source that best represents it. Not a strawman.
Concede what's actually true. The other side has a real point. Name it. Use the source.
Identify the limit. Where does the opposing view break down? What does it fail to account for? Pull in another source to show this.
Use the limit to sharpen your claim. The point isn't to dismiss the other side, but to use the engagement to make your position more precise.

The counterargument that earns Row C
Strong counterargument doesn't just protect your thesis. It strengthens it. The reader walks away thinking "even when this writer engaged the strongest version of the other side, their position got SHARPER." That's the move.



Step 6 — Score yourself
Before you turn in any synthesis essay, check your work against these. If you can answer yes to all of them, you're in 4-6 territory.

Row A (Thesis) — 1 point
Does my thesis take a defensible position, not just summarize the sources?
Does my thesis qualify (acknowledge what's reasonable about the other side)?
Could a reasonable person disagree with what I'm claiming?
Row B (Evidence and Commentary) — up to 4 points
Did I use at least three sources?
Are my sources in conversation, or are they taking turns?
After each source pairing, did I explain what the sources TOGETHER reveal?
Does each body paragraph build on the last, not just repeat the same point?
Are my claims appropriately scoped to what the sources actually demonstrate?
Row C (Sophistication) — 1 point
Did I do something complex with the prompt's terms (qualify, distinguish kinds, name conditions)?
Is that complexity woven through the essay, not just one gesture in the conclusion?

Time management (15 + 40)
Synthesis is the only essay with a 15-minute reading period. Use it. Students who treat the reading period as 'free time' to start writing in their head end up with weaker BEAM mapping and weaker theses.

The 15-minute reading period
0-3 minutes: Read the prompt twice. Identify the position you'll need to take.
3-13 minutes: Read all six sources. Mark BEAM next to each one. Note who the writer is and what their stake is.
13-15 minutes: Sketch your thesis. Pick three sources to use. Decide your line of reasoning pattern.


The 40-minute writing period
0-3 minutes: Refine your thesis. Sketch your two body paragraph claims and which sources go in each.
3-10 minutes: Write the introduction. Land the thesis.
10-32 minutes: Write two body paragraphs (or two body + one counterargument). Use The Synthesis Move in every paragraph.
32-37 minutes: Conclusion. Land the sophistication move. No 'since the beginning of time' gestures.
37-40 minutes: Read back through. Fix any places where sources are taking turns instead of in conversation.


Common mistakes checklist
These are the six patterns that consistently keep students at a 3 or below. Check your own essay against this list before you turn it in.

1. Sources taking turns instead of in conversation
Each source gets its own sentence or paragraph. They never enter dialogue. Fix: use The Synthesis Move. Put two or three sources in the same passage and explain what they together reveal.
2. Commentary that restates
"This is significant because" introduces paraphrase, not analysis. "This illustrates that..." is followed by what the evidence already said. Fix: ask "what would I lose if this source pairing weren't there?" That answer is your commentary.
3. Acknowledge-and-dismiss counterargument
You raise a counterargument and immediately wave it off without engaging. Fix: either genuinely refute it (steel-man, concede, limit, sharpen) or cut it. A half-engaged counterargument hurts your Row B more than no counterargument at all.
4. Overgeneralization
Claims broader than the sources support. "All schools should implement honor codes." "Every region should adopt vertical farming." Fix: scope your claims to what the sources actually demonstrate. Use words like "in contexts where..." or "for populations that..."
5. Personal observation that just sits there
You assert something about MVHS or your own experience but never weave it into the source conversation. Fix: either tie the personal observation directly to a source ("my own experience confirms what Source A documents...") or cut it.
6. Thesis that lists rather than argues
"Although X, Y, and Z prescribe vertical farming, sources A and B prove otherwise." Close, but listy. Fix: pick TWO clear reasons that depend on each other. Show how reason 1 sets up reason 2.
