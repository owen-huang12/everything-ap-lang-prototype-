Step 1 — What is the argument essay?
The argument essay (Q3) gives you a claim from a writer, thinker, or public figure and asks you to argue your own position on it. Unlike rhetorical analysis, you're not analyzing somebody else's writing. You're making your own case using your own evidence.
Two completely different questions:
Restating (NOT argument)
Answers: What does the prompt say?
Repeats the claim back without taking a position.
Example: "Schumacher argues that smallness requires courage."
Argument (YES argument)
Answers: What is YOUR position on the claim, and how do you prove it?
Takes a defensible stance and supports it with specific evidence.
Example: "Schumacher's claim is largely valid in industries where scale produces hidden externalities, but it fails in domains where scale itself produces public good."


What the prompt is actually asking
Every AP Lang Q3 prompt asks the same thing in slightly different words:
The standard prompt
Write an essay that argues your position on the extent to which [the writer's] claim about [topic] is valid.


The phrase "the extent to which" is doing real work. The strongest essays do not flat agree or flat disagree. They qualify: valid in some contexts but not others, valid for some kinds of cases but not others, valid except for one important condition.
How argument is different from the other two essays
Argument (Q3): Make YOUR own argument. Evidence comes from your own knowledge and experience.
RA (Q2): Analyze how an author makes THEIR argument. Evidence comes only from the passage.
Synthesis (Q1): Build an argument using multiple provided sources

Step 2 — Read the prompt for the question underneath
Every Q3 prompt has two layers: the surface (what the writer literally said) and the underneath (the argumentative question that surface is asking you to answer). Most low-scoring essays argue against the surface. High-scoring essays answer the underneath.

Practice with the running example
Schumacher prompt
In 1973, economist E.F. Schumacher wrote: "Any intelligent fool can make things bigger, more complex, and more violent. It takes a touch of genius, and a lot of courage, to move in the opposite direction." Write an essay that argues your position on the value of prioritizing smallness, simplicity, or restraint over growth and expansion.


The two layers
The surface (don't stop here)
Schumacher says smallness takes courage.
Surface response: agree or disagree with whether smallness requires courage.
This will get you a thesis but not much more.
The underneath (this is the real prompt)
Is prioritizing smallness, simplicity, or restraint valuable in our current world?
Real response: under what conditions does smallness produce better outcomes? When does it fail? What kinds of growth are worth resisting?
This is the question you actually have to answer.


Three diagnostic questions for any prompt
What is the surface claim? (What did the writer literally say?)
What is the underlying question? (What argumentative question is the prompt forcing you to take a position on?)
Where does it want me to qualify? (What's the case where the surface claim doesn't hold?)

Step 3 — Brainstorm with PEEPS
The biggest reason students get a 2 in Row B is that their evidence bank is too narrow. They reach for one personal story, one vague historical reference, and stop. PEEPS forces you to brainstorm across five different frames so you walk into your essay with options.

The five frames
P — Psychological: What does this claim suggest about how the human mind works?
E — Economic: Are there market forces, costs, or financial incentives that prove or complicate the claim?
E — Ethical: What moral or values-based dimension is at play?
P — Pragmatic: What are the practical, real-world consequences?
S — Sociological: What does this claim reveal about groups, institutions, or social patterns?

How to actually brainstorm
Spend 3-5 minutes filling in each frame with one or two specific pieces of evidence you actually have. The goal is options, not perfection. Once you have ideas across multiple frames, pick the two strongest for your essay.

Schumacher PEEPS brainstorm (sample)
P — Psychological: Decision fatigue research showing that more options reduce satisfaction.
E — Economic: Fast fashion's externalized costs (labor, environment) hidden by scale.
E — Ethical: Big tech and attention extraction; smaller platforms with different incentive structures.
P — Pragmatic: Ozempic's massive scale producing environmental side effects (the running example from one of our calibration sessions).
S — Sociological: Decline of local journalism vs. national media consolidation.

Step 4 — Plan your essay
Once you have a brainstorm, your essay is mostly built. Pick two or three of your strongest examples, decide your line of reasoning, and write a thesis that actually contains the architecture of your argument.
One of the most consistent patterns in low-scoring essays: students try to cram in three or four examples and develop none of them. The 1-4-1 anchor essays from College Board often use just two examples. Two specific, fully developed examples beat four name-dropped ones every time.

Decide your structure
Weakest to strongest: Build momentum across paragraphs. Save your most convincing example for last.
Concession to refutation: Open with what's true about the other side, then dismantle it with your strongest case.
Cause to effect: Show how one principle plays out across multiple cases.

Write a thesis that contains the architecture
The argument thesis formula
Although [concession: what's reasonable about the other side], [your position], because [reason 1] and [reason 2].


Evidence quality: high vs. low leverage
Not all evidence is equal. The same paragraph can score a 2 or a 4 depending entirely on what you put in the evidence slot. The single biggest predictor of Row B scores is whether your evidence is specific enough to do real argumentative work.
High-leverage evidence
Named historical events, figures, dates, places. ('Fannie Lou Hamer's 1964 testimony before the DNC credentials committee.')
Documented social or political phenomena with specifics. ('The 2008 collapse of regional newspapers in the U.S.')
Verifiable data, studies, or research with attribution. ('A 2023 Pew study found that 71% of teens...')
Specific cultural texts, films, books, court cases that you can name and quote.
Low-leverage evidence
Personal anecdote alone. ('My friend once...') Personal anecdote can support a claim, but cannot anchor a paragraph by itself.
Vague generalizations. ('Many people believe...' 'Studies show...' without specifics.)
Hypothetical scenarios without grounding. ('Imagine someone who...')
Made-up sources or invented statistics. (Readers will notice. It actively hurts your score.)
The specificity test
Before you commit to an example
Could a reader who has never met me verify this with a quick search? If yes, it's high-leverage. If no, it's personal anecdote and needs to be paired with something verifiable.


Thesis formula + thesis surgery
A working template for the argument thesis. Use it as scaffolding. Loosen it as you get stronger.
The formula
Although [concession: what's reasonable about the other side], [your position], because [reason 1] and [reason 2].

What each piece does
Concession: Names the strongest version of the opposing view. Forces you to engage complexity from sentence one.
Your position: A defensible claim, not a restatement. Should be qualified, not flat.
Reasons: Each reason becomes a body paragraph. Two is ideal, three at most.
The four thesis illnesses (remember we did a worksheet on this?) 
These are the patterns that consistently lose the Row A point or set up a weak essay. Diagnose your draft thesis against this list before you write.
Illness #1: The Grocery List
Symptom: Lists reasons without showing how they connect. "Schumacher is right because of the environment, the economy, and human happiness."
Cure: Make the reasons depend on each other. Use 'because,' 'which produces,' 'leading to.'


Illness #2: The Topic Sentence
Symptom: Names the topic without taking a position. "The value of smallness has been debated for centuries."
Cure: Finish the sentence. What's YOUR claim? Not 'has been debated' but 'is X for these reasons.'


Illness #3: The Almost
Symptom: Has a concession but stops before explaining the mechanism. "While growth has benefits, smallness is better."
Cure: Add the "because." A concession is not enough. The reader needs the mechanism.


Illness #4: The Shrug
Symptom: Acknowledges both sides and refuses to land. "Both growth and smallness have value, and the answer depends on context."
Cure: Pick a side. Even a qualified position is better than no position. The rubric requires a defensible claim, and "it depends" is not a claim.

Three thesis levels (Schumacher example)
Same prompt, three versions of the thesis, each more developed than the last. Use the level you can confidently sustain in your essay. A clean Level 1 thesis with strong commentary scores higher than a tangled Level 3 thesis you cannot back up.
Level 1 — Basic (earns Row A)
Sample: "Schumacher is correct that prioritizing smallness over growth produces better outcomes, because smaller systems are more accountable and more sustainable."
What it does: Takes a clear, defensible position and names two reasons. Earns the Row A point. Gives you a body-paragraph map (one paragraph per reason).
Why it works: Defensible. Specific reasons. Not a restatement. Most students can write this version under time pressure.
Sentence frame: 
[Writer] is correct that [surface claim, restated as a position], because [reason 1] and [reason 2].


Level 2 — Developed (sets up Row B 3-4)
Sample: "Although Schumacher overstates the universal value of smallness, his core claim holds in domains where scale produces hidden externalities the smaller version did not, because the costs of bigness are typically displaced onto people, ecosystems, or futures that cannot bargain back."
What it does: Concedes (Schumacher overstates), qualifies (only in specific domains), and names the mechanism (displaced costs). Now the thesis is doing analytical work, not just listing.
Why it works: The qualifier creates a real argument. Body paragraphs can explore which domains qualify. Commentary will naturally explain mechanisms instead of restating.
Sentence frame: 
Although [concession: what's reasonable about the opposing view], [qualified position: where/when claim holds], because [mechanism that explains why].


Level 3 — Sophisticated (primes Row C)
Sample: "Schumacher's claim is most useful not as a verdict on smallness vs. growth, but as a diagnostic: it asks us to name what scale is hiding. In domains where bigness externalizes its costs onto bystanders (pharmaceutical waste, fast fashion labor, attention extraction), Schumacher is right that restraint requires courage. But in domains where scale itself produces public goods (vaccines, basic research, infrastructure), the same instinct toward smallness becomes a refusal to share. The genius Schumacher names is not in choosing smallness, but in knowing when scale serves and when it conceals."
What it does: Reframes the prompt itself. Doesn't argue Schumacher is right or wrong. Argues his claim is a tool for distinguishing two kinds of cases. That distinction can be sustained throughout the essay.
Why it works: The complexity is built into the thesis. Body paragraphs naturally inherit it. Row C tends to follow when this kind of thesis is sustained across the whole essay.
Warning: Only attempt this if you can sustain it across all your body paragraphs. A Level 3 thesis with Level 1 commentary will hurt your score, not help it.
Sentence frame: 
[Writer's] claim is most useful not as [the simple reading], but as [a more nuanced reframe]. In domains where [condition 1], the claim holds, but in domains where [condition 2], [the same logic produces a different outcome].

How to choose your level
Pick Level 1 if: you're short on time, the prompt is unfamiliar, or you're not yet comfortable with concession-based theses.
Pick Level 2 if: you can clearly articulate where the claim fails and where it holds, and you have time to develop the mechanism in body paragraphs.
Pick Level 3 if: you've identified a genuine reframe (a way of seeing the prompt that goes beyond agree/disagree), and you're confident you can sustain that complexity through every paragraph.

Step 5 — Write the essay
This is where most students lose Row B points. Not because they can't write, but because their commentary stops at restatement. Don't do that.

The body paragraph formula
Topic sentence — name the claim and connect to your thesis.
Embed evidence — specific, named, verifiable. Not 'a civil rights leader' but 'Fannie Lou Hamer's 1964 DNC testimony.'
Close the loop — explain HOW the evidence proves your claim, not just THAT it does.
Concluding sentence — tie back to your thesis and set up the next paragraph.

The HOW Loop (the move that wins Row B)
The formula
This evidence proves [claim] because [mechanism], which matters because [stakes].


Weak vs. strong commentary
Weak (Row B 1-2)
The Ozempic example shows that bigger isn't always better. Schumacher would agree that this proves his point about scale being harmful.
Names the example. Restates the thesis. Doesn't explain the mechanism or the stakes.
Strong (Row B 3-4)
Ozempic's mass production of semaglutide proves Schumacher's claim because the scale itself created a problem the smaller version never had: amphibians downstream of pharmaceutical wastewater experience insulin spikes that disrupt their metabolism. This matters because it reveals scale as a hidden cost mechanism, where bigness produces externalities the original small-scale use did not.
Names the choice. Explains the mechanism (scale to wastewater to disruption). Names the stakes (hidden cost mechanism). Closes the loop.


How to handle the conclusion
The conclusion is where many strong essays go off the rails. Stay tethered to your argument. Do NOT:
Drift into "since the beginning of time..." generalizations.
Tell a personal story that doesn't connect to your thesis.
Hedge with 'in the end, both sides have a point.'

Instead: return to your thesis with new clarity. The conclusion is your last chance to land a sophistication move.
Body paragraph scaffold + the HOW Loop
Row B is where the score is won or lost. Most students lose points here because their commentary stops at restating the thesis. The HOW Loop is the move that fixes that.
The body paragraph in 4 parts
Topic sentence — name the claim and link to your thesis.
Evidence — specific, named, verifiable.
Commentary that closes the HOW Loop — explain the mechanism, not the connection.
Concluding sentence — tie back to your thesis, set up the next paragraph.

Three commentary moves to escape the closed loop
If you find yourself writing 'this proves my thesis' in three different ways, you're in the closed loop. Use these sentence starters to break out:
Specifically, what this evidence shows is...
The reason this matters is that...
What this reveals about [topic] is ...
This works because ...
Which means that...
Weak vs. strong, side by side Another example 
Row B 1-2: Restates and stops
"Ozempic is a perfect example of how growth creates problems. The drug shows that bigger is not always better. This proves Schumacher's point that scale brings consequences."
Names the example. Restates the thesis. Doesn't explain the mechanism. The reader still doesn't know HOW Ozempic proves anything.
Row B 3-4: Closes the HOW Loop
"Ozempic's mass production proves Schumacher's claim because scaling semaglutide production created a wastewater stream of pharmaceutical residue that disrupts amphibian metabolism downstream of treatment plants. Smaller-scale therapeutic use never produced this externality. This matters because it reveals scale itself as the cost mechanism: the harm did not exist at small scale and was generated by the act of scaling."
Names the mechanism (scale → wastewater → disruption). Names the stakes (scale as cost mechanism). Closes the loop.


The three moves of strong commentary
1. Name a specific mechanism. Not 'this proves my thesis.' A precise causal step.
2. Show the gears turning. Walk the reader through the because-the-which-means chain.
3. Connect to stakes. Why does this matter beyond this one example?
Counterargument: how to actually do it
Counterargument is the most misunderstood move in argument writing. Done well, it's a path to Row C. Done poorly, it actively HURTS your Row B score by suggesting your argument has a hole you couldn't fill.
The three placement options
Setup (in your intro or BP1): Concede the strongest opposing view, then pivot to your position. Sets up your essay's complexity from the start.
Embedded (within a body paragraph): Concede a specific point, then refute it within the same paragraph. Tightest version.
Standalone paragraph: Dedicate a full paragraph to the strongest opposing view, then refute it. Most common, but riskiest if you don't actually refute.
WHAT NOT TO DO
DON'T DO: Concede and Drop
"Some might argue smallness is impractical for global problems. However, smallness is still better."
Raises the counterargument. Doesn't engage it. Leaves a hole in your line of reasoning. Hurts Row B.
DON'T DO: Define Your Way Out
"For the sake of this essay, I'm only counting the cases where smallness produces good outcomes."
Circular. You're saying you'll only count the evidence that proves your point. Readers see this immediately.


How to refute well
Steel-man it. State the strongest version of the opposing view. Not a strawman.
Concede what's actually true. The other side has a real point. Name it.
Identify the limit. Where does the opposing view break down? What does it fail to account for?
Use the limit to sharpen your claim. The point isn't to dismiss the other side, but to use the engagement to make your position more precise.
The counterargument that earns Row C
Strong counterargument doesn't just protect your thesis. It strengthens it. The reader walks away thinking "even when this writer engaged the strongest version of the other side, their position got SHARPER." That's the move.


Sophistication: how to earn Row C
Row C is rare and hard to earn. Don't chase it with a sweeping opener. Sophistication tends to come naturally when your argument is precise and your engagement with complexity is genuine.
What does NOT earn Row C
A "since the beginning of time..." opener.
One impressive sentence in an otherwise flat essay.
Acknowledging a counterargument and dismissing it without engagement.
'Both sides have a point' hedging.
The four cleanest paths to Row C
Path 1: Distinguish kinds
Show that the prompt's key term contains multiple meanings, and your argument depends on which one. "Schumacher's claim holds for extractive growth but not generative growth."


Path 2: Name conditions
Identify when the claim works and when it doesn't. Sustain that distinction across the essay. "Fear works as persuasion when stakes are immediate, fails when they're long-term."


Path 3: Synthesize across examples
Don't just give three parallel proofs. Use your examples to build toward a unified insight that none of them could prove alone.


Path 4: Weaponize the counterargument
Take the strongest version of the opposing view and use it to sharpen your own claim, not just protect it. The reader should finish your essay thinking your argument got SHARPER from the engagement.


The Row C test
Ask yourself: did my essay say something a reader couldn't have predicted from the prompt alone? If yes, you're in Row C territory. If your argument is exactly what the prompt set up, no matter how well-written, you're not.

Step 6 — Score yourself
Before you turn in any argument essay, check your work against these. If you can answer yes to all of them, you're in 4-6 territory.

Row A (Thesis) — 1 point
Does my thesis take a defensible position, not just restate the prompt?
Does my thesis qualify (engage the 'extent to which' framing)?
Could a reasonable person disagree with what I'm claiming?
Row B (Evidence and Commentary) — up to 4 points
Is my evidence specific, named, and verifiable (not 'studies show' or 'a civil rights leader')?
After each piece of evidence, did I explain HOW it proves my claim, not just THAT it does?
Does each body paragraph build on the last, not just repeat the same point?
Did I avoid hedging language ('often,' 'sometimes,' 'in the end')?
If I raised a counterargument, did I genuinely refute it (not just 'concede and move on')?
Row C (Sophistication) — 1 point
Did I do something complex with the prompt's terms (qualify, distinguish kinds, name conditions)?
Is that complexity woven through the essay, not just one gesture in the conclusion?.

Time management on test day
You have 40 minutes for this essay
0-5 minutes: Read the prompt twice. Identify the surface and the underneath.
5-12 minutes: PEEPS brainstorm. Pick your two strongest examples.
12-17 minutes: Write thesis using the formula. Sketch your two body paragraph claims.
17-37 minutes: Write your intro, two body paragraphs, and counterargument. Close the HOW Loop in every paragraph.
37-40 minutes: Conclusion. Land the sophistication move. No 'since the beginning of time' gestures.
